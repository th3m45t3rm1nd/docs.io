**Status:** Accepted  
**Decides:** Business model, inventory ownership, fulfillment responsibility

## Context

A construction materials platform can operate in three models:

1. **First-party (1P):** Platform buys stock, stores in warehouse, ships to customers. Full control, high capital requirement.
    
2. **Marketplace:** Suppliers list products, fulfill their own orders. Platform takes commission. Low capital, less control.
    
3. **Hybrid:** Platform stocks high-velocity items, suppliers list everything else. Most complex to operate.
    

We needed to choose one for the initial build. This decision affects the database schema, order flow, supplier dashboard scope, GST compliance obligations, and payout logic fundamentally.

---

## Decision

**Pure marketplace model.** No warehouse. No platform-owned inventory.

Suppliers onboard, upload their catalog, set their prices, and fulfill orders using their own vehicles or delivery arrangements. The platform facilitates discovery, payment collection, and trust.

---

## Reasoning

**Capital efficiency at launch:** Stocking cement, steel, and bricks in a Delhi warehouse requires significant working capital locked in slow-moving inventory. The marketplace model requires near-zero inventory capital, allowing the engineering and operations budget to go toward product and supplier acquisition instead.

**Supplier relationships first:** Delhi NCR has an established network of building material dealers, hardware distributors, and steel stockists. These suppliers already have their own delivery infrastructure for their existing wholesale customers. Plugging into their existing operations (rather than replacing them) is faster to launch.

**Validation before commitment:** The marketplace model lets us validate demand, pricing, and supplier reliability before committing capital to 1P inventory. If a product category proves high-volume and margin-positive, we can consider 1P for that category specifically at a later stage.

---

## Schema implications

This decision requires the following tables that would not exist in a 1P model:

- `suppliers` — extended profile for verified seller accounts
- `supplier_products` — the listing layer (same product, multiple suppliers, different prices and stock levels)
- `sub_orders` — one parent order splits into N sub-orders, one per supplier involved in a checkout
- `supplier_payouts` — settlement records per sub-order
- `supplier_documents` — GSTIN certificate, PAN, trade license stored for verification

The `products` table holds the shared product definition (name, HSN code, category, attributes). The `supplier_products` table holds the supplier-specific listing (price, stock, lead time, minimum order quantity).

---

## GST obligation under this model

As a marketplace aggregator, the platform must collect Tax Collected at Source (TCS) at 1% on the net value of taxable supplies made through it, under Section 52 of the CGST Act 2017.

This TCS is deducted from supplier payouts and remitted to the government by the platform in GSTR-8 monthly.

Suppliers receive their payout net of platform commission and TCS. They can claim TCS credit in their own GST returns.

This is accounted for in the `supplier_payouts` table via the `tcs_deducted_paise` column.

## Consequences

- Platform has less control over delivery quality and timelines compared to 1P — supplier SLA enforcement is an operations problem, not just a schema problem
- Customer experience depends heavily on supplier reliability — supplier rating and review systems are important from day one
- TCS compliance adds monthly filing obligation (GSTR-8)
- Order splitting logic is the most complex part of the backend — a cart with 3 items from 3 suppliers creates 1 order + 3 sub-orders + 3 payout records in a single transaction
- Returns are more complex: the customer returns to the supplier directly, not to a platform warehouse
